Development of The Blue Screen

By Aidan Graef

Introduction

        The Blue Screen is a game my team and I developed for the Brackeys 2023.2 Game Jam. In this game jam, teams competed to fully develop an original video game. The game and all its assets must be created from scratch - all art, music, sound, and code must be created by the team. Additionally, teams only have one week to create their games. This year’s game jam had 915 entries with over 19,000 ratings. Our game placed 10th overall, with an average rating of 4 out of 5 stars.

Ideation

        Each game jam begins the same way: with the announcement of the theme. As soon as we learned the theme was “Diving Deeper”, we immediately began discussing ideas for our submission.

        In a contest like this, it was important to us that our game stands out from other submissions, so our first order of business was to determine what we thought other teams would create. We came to the conclusion that, when presented with the theme “Diving Deeper”, most teams would create an underwater game. When the week of development was over, we would discover our assumption was correct - roughly ninety percent of submissions were water-based games.

        To make our game stand out, we needed to use “Diving Deeper” in a different way. I suggested that we could interpret it less literally, and make a detective game where the player can dive into characters’ minds and view their memories to solve the case. While we had many different ideas, this one quickly became the most popular, and we took a vote at the end of the first day. We had decided that we would make a detective game.

        We now had to split our team into roles. We ended up with one person on music, one on sound effects, and one full-time programmer. Meanwhile, myself and one other teammate pivoted between the roles of programming and art as needed. Lastly, since we were making a largely story-based game, I was also the team’s main writer, and our full-time programmer stepped in occasionally as editor.

Early Design Decisions

        It is always important to weigh design decisions carefully, as they can have a large impact on future development. This is even more critical with the heavy one-week time restriction imposed by the game jam timeline. A lot of discussion went into each of the decisions we made, so I will summarize some of the most important decisions, as well as the reasoning behind them.

  • 2D - The scope of a game jam game must be small - a well-designed submission can usually be finished in about 15 minutes. The most important elements of our game would be its characters, story, and dialogue. While a 3D environment can offer certain advantages, such as exploration or realism, we felt it would only clutter the vision we had for our game.
  • Setting - As discussed above, we chose our setting based on what would stand out as different from other submissions. We decided our characters would be human-like androids in a futuristic city, and we made the game take place inside of a dive bar to further fit with the theme.
  • Pixel Art - Choosing a good art style for your game is important, as it conveys to the player what type of game to expect. Pixel art games are often either number-crunching turn-based RPGs or calm, character-driven narratives. We aimed for the latter and avoided the former by increasing our pixel count.
  • Scene Count - The number of scenes in your game is often a significant factor in determining workload, and single-scene games are very popular in game jams. We decided that, while the whole story will take place in a single scene, we should separate out our main game mechanic, memory diving, into a separate scene. This allows the player to focus on completing the “game” part of our game without worrying about the story, and lets them focus on the story without worrying about playing the game correctly.
  • Music & Sound - These keep the player grounded in the game. We decided to have a separate music track for viewing memories to distinguish them from the present-day events. Also, our brilliant sound designer had the amazing idea to give every character a different instrument sound effect when speaking, to differentiate them from each other, and to establish their personality and tone when they speak.

Mechanics

        Mechanics are what makes a video game a video game. In a story-driven game, it is important that the mechanics reinforce what is happening in the story. Our main game mechanic became “Diving”. Whenever a character said something fishy or partially untrue to the player, they would have the option to dive into that character’s memories to uncover the truth. To simulate being in that character’s head, we had a large amount of “thoughts” bouncing around.

        Clicking on the wrong thought bubble simply displays an irrelevant thought in the character’s head(e.g. “I wonder what I should get for lunch?” or “Doesn’t that new movie come out today?”). Clicking on the truest emotion allows the player to see into the character’s memory of a past event, which sheds light on what happened the night of the crime.

        To increase difficulty with progression, the amount of distracting thoughts increases with every new memory viewed. You are free to view memories you’ve already seen multiple times, but this doesn’t increase the difficulty. In total there are thirteen memories - three for each suspect, and one special memory, viewable by accessing the murdered android’s memory chip.

        Lastly, we used a complex dialogue system to create branching trees of dialogue. This allowed us to give the player the choice of what they would like to ask the characters. Player choice is essential in narrative video games, as it is what prevents your game from turning into a low-graphics movie. We made these choices have consequences by linking the dive-able memories to the choices the player made. To make this work, we needed to prepare for the situation where a player doesn’t find all memories. While it is possible to view every memory in a single play-through, and every memory does help you uncover the truth, only four memories are necessary for completing the game.

The Story

        Our game was a story game - the players’ (and our) success hinged on their ability to successfully discover the culprit and unravel the mystery. We needed to somehow make talking to characters both fun on its own and productive towards discovering the culprit.

        After clearly defining our story goals, I wrote the first draft of our story.

        The story was a hit with the team, and I began refining the details and writing in-game dialogue with input from the team. Eventually, one of our team members suggested a surprising twist: that the player’s character was the culprit!

        While this was an amazing idea, it obviously posed some difficulties - if the players saw that they could select themselves as the culprit, they would almost always choose that option out of curiosity. And if we only unlocked that option after they had played enough of the game to discover it, it would clearly be the correct answer, eliminating the mystery.

        After some discussion, we decided that this twist was worth taking on. We got to work solving these problems. Our final solution involved the player in the crime, but he/she was NOT the ultimate culprit. We kept the surprise without losing the mystery.

We then divided work according to our skill sets, with my teammate on character design and myself on environment and UI. Lessons Learned

        Creating The Blue Screen taught me many lessons about game development, and I am incredibly thankful for the opportunity to participate in this event. Below are some of the insights I gained from working on this project:

  • Make important design decisions early - Early choices often cascade and affect later work. For example, our in-game camera and our canvas size were made specifically for pixel art. If you don’t think about the future when making these decisions, or don’t give yourself enough time to adjust if you have to, then you will be stuck with problems you don’t have time to fix. We did run into this issue a bit (our single-scene choice meant programming memories was somewhat difficult) but for the most part, our later work benefitted from our earlier decisions.
  • Don’t get too committed too quickly - We had a teammate suggest a great idea for the story after we had already written most of it. If we had gotten too committed to the work we had already done, we would've missed out on the great surprise twist our game now has. You have to be flexible enough to allow for new ideas to thrive.
  • Talk,  talk, and talk - It is very common for two people to have different images in their head of what the project is supposed to look like. From time to time, this could cause issues (Our sound designer did not have a sound effect for the player character because they did not know they would have a speaking part in one memory). It is important to stay in constant communication with teammates even when you aren’t working on the same task.
  • Listen, listen, listen - Just as important, if not more, is to actively listen to each other. Your teammates likely have amazing ideas that you didn’t think of.
  • Plan your work ahead - As a team, we used a kanban board to keep track of what work needed to be done. This helped us keep the project within scope, instead of having too many ideas and no time to implement them.
